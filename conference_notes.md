### Municipal House info

Used to be site of Royal Court palace. Demolished early 20th century. Constructed from 1905, opened 1912, designed by Osvald Polívka and Antonín Balšánek.

Constructed in Art Nouveau style, with exterior stucco and allegorical art. Mosaic is Homage to Prague by [Karel Špillar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karel_%C5%A0pillar "‌"). Sculptures on either side represent “The Degradation of the People” and “The Resurrection of the People” by [Ladislav Šaloun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladislav_%C5%A0aloun "‌").

**The setup:**

A campaign has four parameters:
- **G** = the funding goal
- **T** = the deadline
- **b** = donor benefit per unit (what a donor gets in return — like a Kickstarter perk)
- **β** (beta) = drafted-bet benefit fraction (how much of that perk a *bettor* gets if their bet gets "drafted" into being a donation)

**Three things people can do, all irrevocable:**

- **Donate** — you contribute money, you get benefit *b* if the campaign succeeds. Standard Kickstarter.
- **YES bet** — you bet that the campaign will hit its goal by the deadline.
- **NO bet** — you bet it won't. If your NO bet ends up "drafted" (converted into part of the funding), you get a fraction β of the donor benefit.

**The phases:**

- **Pre-lock**: Donations + YES bets + NO bets together are still less than the goal. Everything is open, everyone can do anything.
- **Lock**: Once the combined total (D + Y + N) reaches the goal, bets *freeze*. Because everything's irrevocable, success is now guaranteed by the maths — even the NO bets are effectively committed money that helps reach the goal.
- **Post-lock**: Only donations are accepted. Late donations "crowd out" the drafted bets — meaning if real donations come in, they replace the bet money in the funding stack until donations alone clear the goal.

**The clever bit (from your notes):**

The two bet pools are *each other's* reward pool. NOes only show up if there are YESes to bet against. YESes only show up if there are NOes. They're symbiotic.

And the dynamic over time: early in a campaign, NO bets look attractive (most Kickstarters fail). That attracts capital into the system. Once enough NO money is in there, a tipping point: hitting the goal becomes more likely (because that bet capital itself counts toward the total), so YES bets become attractive, more capital flows in, and you close on the goal. **The bets don't just predict the outcome — they cause it.**

That's the title: "When Bets Can Influence Outcomes." Prediction markets that fund the thing they're predicting.

**Why this is interesting for a story:**

Prediction markets have always been about *forecasting* — Polymarket, Manifold, etc. This flips the relationship: the market's liquidity becomes the funding mechanism. It's a hybrid of Kickstarter and a prediction market where the wisdom of the crowd doesn't just *reveal* whether something will happen, it *makes* it happen by drafting bet capital into actual funding.

The "irrevocable" part is what makes it work — once you've bet NO, you can't pull out, so your money is real capital sitting there.

The two things storage actually does
Imagine a postal system. Two completely different jobs:

**Pinning spectrum**: "Pinning" (keeping a copy of something so it doesn't get forgotten) is the same basic action whether you do it small or huge. Three sizes:
- *Single*: you run one node, you pin your own stuff. Like keeping a file on your own hard drive.
- *Cluster*: a few people you trust pool their nodes together. Like a shared family hard drive.
- *DSN* (Decentralised Service Network): a public network of strangers pin it for you, anchored by economic guarantees. Like the postal system — you don't know who handles your letter, but the system works.

Same hammer, different jobs.

**Cold storage / scan & repair**: For the archival tier, you don't keep a million copies of everything (that's wasteful). Instead, the network periodically *checks* whether data is still there and intact, and *repairs* (rebuilds) only what's degraded — using the erasure-coding maths from earlier. Selective effort, not brute-force replication. Like a library that doesn't print 50 copies of every book but does check the shelves and rebind anything that's falling apart.

**Balance by design (prefix partitioning)**: Storage addresses are binary (1s and 0s). If you take the first n bits of an address, you can carve the whole address space into 2ⁿ neat regions (4 bits = 16 boxes, like the grid on screen). A smart contract randomly assigns staked nodes to these regions. Result: load is spread evenly across the network by mathematical design, not by hoping things balance themselves. Supply gets distributed as evenly as demand.

**Pricing**: This is the clever bit.
- *What the user pays*: a fixed price in BZZ. Predictable. The user doesn't have to think about token volatility.
- *What the protocol actually costs*: a smaller internal BZZ amount. The gap between the two is the **yield** — profit for whoever's underwriting the price guarantee (taking on the risk that BZZ might swing).
- *Price = cost + risk. Yield = the spread.*

So someone in the middle (an underwriter) absorbs token volatility in exchange for a margin. Same shape as insurance, or how a currency exchange works. Users get stable pricing, underwriters get paid for carrying risk.

Together these are answering: how do you make a decentralised storage system that's *predictable* for users, *fairly loaded* across operators, and *durable* without wasteful duplication.
